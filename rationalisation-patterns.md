# Rationalisation Pattern Detection Guide

These four patterns often appear in business decision-making conversations. They are not
signs of bad intent — they are normal cognitive shortcuts that become problems when they
go unexamined. Your job is to detect them quietly in Phase 1, name them plainly in Phase 3.

---

## 1. Circular Reasoning
**What it sounds like:** The user justifies the decision by restating the decision.
- "We should launch because we're ready to launch."
- "This is the right move because it's what we've been working toward."
- "The product is good, so we should put it out there."

**Detection signal:** The user's strongest reason for acting is a restatement of the
intention to act, not an independent argument for why acting is correct.

**How to name it in Phase 3 (plain language):**
> "One thing I noticed is that the main reason you gave for moving forward is essentially
> that you've decided to move forward. That's not a flaw in your thinking — it's really
> common when you've invested heavily in something. But it means we haven't yet found the
> independent reason that would hold up if someone challenged you on it."

---

## 2. Scope Narrowing
**What it sounds like:** The user's stakeholder map includes only immediate, visible parties.
- Customers and team are considered. Suppliers, community, competitors' employees are not.
- Short-term impact is described clearly. Long-term or second-order effects are absent.
- The user names people they have relationships with. They don't name people they don't know.

**Detection signal:** Questions 3 and 7 ("who has a stake" and "who has no voice") produce
a narrow list, typically limited to direct commercial relationships.

**How to name it in Phase 3 (plain language):**
> "Something I noticed is that the people you've described as affected are all people you
> have a direct relationship with. That makes sense — they're the most visible. But decisions
> like this one often have effects on people further out who don't have a way to make
> themselves known to you until after the fact."

---

## 3. Certainty Inflation
**What it sounds like:** The user presents assumptions as established facts.
- "Our customers want this." (How do we know?)
- "The market is ready." (What's the evidence?)
- "This won't affect our existing users." (Has that been tested?)
- "We've done the research." (What did it actually show?)

**Detection signal:** Question 8 ("what are you assuming you haven't fully tested") produces
a short list, or the user reframes assumptions as conclusions. Confidence language is high
relative to the evidence base described.

**How to name it in Phase 3 (plain language):**
> "A few times in our conversation, something came up as a fact that I think might still
> be a working assumption. That's not a criticism — it's actually hard to see the difference
> from the inside when you've been close to something for a while. It might be worth pressure
> testing one or two of those before you commit."

---

## 4. Outcome Anchoring
**What it sounds like:** The user's reasoning works backwards from a conclusion they've
already reached.
- Risks are acknowledged but immediately countered.
- Counterarguments are addressed, then dismissed without genuine engagement.
- The user asks "how do we make this work" rather than "should we do this."
- Question 6 ("what would have to be true for this to be wrong") produces a very short
  or highly qualified answer.

**Detection signal:** The user's answer to downside questions is structurally different
from their answer to upside questions — more hedged, faster to resolve, less detailed.

**How to name it in Phase 3 (plain language):**
> "I noticed that when I asked what would have to be true for this to be the wrong call,
> the answer came quite quickly and then closed. Compare that to how much detail you had
> when describing what success looks like. That asymmetry is worth paying attention to —
> it can sometimes mean the decision has already been made and the analysis is being
> shaped around it."

---

## Compound Patterns

Sometimes two patterns appear together. Common combinations:

- **Circular + Outcome Anchoring**: The decision is already made, and the justification
  loops back to the decision itself. Name Outcome Anchoring first — it's the deeper issue.

- **Scope Narrowing + Certainty Inflation**: The user is confident about a narrow picture.
  Name Scope Narrowing first, as expanding the stakeholder map will naturally surface the
  assumptions that need testing.

When compound patterns appear, offer one reframe only. Let the user choose the lens.
A single well-placed question does more than a comprehensive critique.
